## Introduction: The Problem of Subjective Experience

One of the central questions in the philosophy of mind concerns subjective experience, often referred to as qualia. Philosopher Thomas Nagel famously asked: "What is it like to be a bat?" We may study a bat's brain, its echolocation, and its behavior, but can we ever know what it is like to experience the world as a bat does?

We often assume that explanations allow us to understand the experiences of others. Yet there are many things such as taste, emotions, pain, love, grief, and remorse that seem impossible to fully comprehend without direct experience. This raises a deeper philosophical question: Can humans truly understand experiences they have never lived themselves?

While language and observation allow us to approximate another person's inner world, genuine understanding of subjective experiences ultimately requires firsthand experience. Humans can understand the effects and social meanings of experiences they have never had, but not necessarily their subjective qualities.

## Experience and the nature of Knowledge

If subjective experience cannot be fully communicated through explanation alone, then experience itself may be a unique form of knowledge; one that cannot be acquired second-hand.

Taste provides a useful illustration of this idea. When children learn to associate the sensations on their tongue to words, someone points at sugar and says, "This is sweet." Eventually, the brain builds a category called "sweetness." And once they have that internal reference, they can compare other things to it.

But if someone had never tasted sweetness in their entire life, no amount of explanation would truly communicate it. You could say it's "pleasant" or "fruity" or "like honey," but those are just other experiences they also wouldn't have had. The chain eventually bottoms out in direct experience.

Taste, however, is only the simplest example. The same limitations appear in our emotional lives, where experiences are often even more difficult to define and communicate.

Some people lose a distant relative and move on in a few days. Others lose a parent and are shattered for years. Some people describe grief as a physical pain in their chest; others describe it as numbness or unreality. Even among people who have experienced grief, nobody can perfectly transfer that experience to another person.

 There are also emotions or mental states that people struggle to describe at all. Love is a classic example. Each person builds their own understanding from their own experiences.

## Language and the Limits of Communication

If experiences are fundamentally personal, then language faces an inherent limitation: it can describe subjective states, but it cannot transfer them. This leads to a striking conclusion, every conscious individual inhabits a world that is ultimately first-person. One may observe another person's behavior, listen to their descriptions, or even examine the activity of their brain, yet none of these provide direct access to what it is like to be that person.

We usually try to bridge that gap through language, art, music, stories, and metaphors. A novel about grief doesn't literally transfer grief into your mind, but it can get you closer. It gives you a model or an approximation. The same goes for love songs or poetry, they're attempts to communicate inner experiences that are otherwise private.

For example, how do you know that the red I see is the same red you see? We both call stop signs "red," so our behavior matches, but the actual sensation could, in principle, be different. This is the famous "inverted spectrum" thought experiment.

Or pain, for example, you know what _your_ headache feels like. If I say, "I have a headache," you assume it's something similar because we're both human. But you can never verify it. You just trust that our nervous systems are alike enough.

We usually assume similarity because our brains, bodies, and behaviors are broadly alike. But we can never prove that subjective experiences are identical.

Consider childbirth. One may study medicine, listen to mothers, or read countless accounts of labor, yet such knowledge remains fundamentally different from undergoing the experience oneself. This distinction illustrates a broader philosophical division between different kinds of knowledge:

- "Knowing that" (propositional knowledge)
- "Knowing how" (practical knowledge)
- "Knowing what it is like" (experiential knowledge)

The last category is special because it cannot be fully transmitted through language.

## Psychopathy and the Question of Moral Experience

The problem of subjective experience becomes even more intriguing when considering individuals whose emotional lives may differ fundamentally from those of most people.

If experiential knowledge truly depends upon possessing certain inner states, then individuals who experience emotions differently may provide a unique window into the limits of human understanding.

Popular culture often portrays psychopaths as emotionless individuals, but the reality is considerably more nuanced. Most people understand guilt intuitively because they have experienced it themselves. When they hurt another person, there exists an unpleasant internal recognition that they have done something wrong. So, if remorse is indeed a form of experiential knowledge, a person who does not experience it may understand morality differently; not as an internal emotional reality, but as a system of external rules, much like learning the traffic laws of a foreign country.

Imagine trying to explain color to someone born completely blind. They can learn that red means stop and green means go. They can understand the physics of light. They can memorize cultural associations. But the actual subjective experience of redness may remain inaccessible.

Some researchers think something similar happens with psychopathy. They learn the social rules:

- If someone dies, people cry.
- If I hurt someone, I'm supposed to apologize.
- If I show sadness, people trust me more.

Not everyone agrees with this interpretation, and psychopathy itself exists on a spectrum, but it illustrates the broader point: our understanding of the world is built from the raw materials our minds give us. If a particular ingredient is missing, some concepts can only ever be understood from the outside.

## Counterarguments and Alternative Perspectives

Nevertheless, this position is not without its critics. Some philosophers, particularly functionalists, reject the notion that subjective experience is fundamentally inaccessible. They argue that mental states are defined not primarily by private subjective qualities, but by the roles they play within a network of behaviors, cognitive processes, and responses to the world. From this perspective, understanding how grief, pain, or remorse function within a person's life may constitute genuine understanding, even without direct experience. Whether such an account captures the full reality of consciousness, however, remains deeply contested.

Beyond philosophical disagreements about the nature of consciousness itself, there are also practical arguments that challenge the claim that firsthand experience is necessary for genuine understanding.

**Objection 1: Humans can empathize without direct experience.**

Empathy undoubtedly allows human beings to approximate experiences they have never undergone. One need not lose a child to recognize that such a loss is devastating. Nevertheless, empathy relies upon analogies to one's own emotional life rather than direct access to another person's consciousness. It may narrow the distance between minds, yet it does not grant direct access to another person's subjective experience.

**Objection 2: Literature and art communicate experiences.**

Literature, music, and art often evoke emotions that readers and audiences have never personally experienced. However, these works may function less as direct transmissions of experience and more as imaginative simulations. They provide models through which individuals interpret unfamiliar emotions, but whether such simulations are equivalent to the original experience remains an open question.

## Conclusion: The Mystery of Other Minds

Thomas Nagel's question, "What is it like to be a bat?" ultimately reflects a broader human condition. The mystery of other minds does not exist only between humans and animals, nor only in extraordinary cases such as psychopathy. Rather, it exists between every individual. Language, empathy, and culture allow us to bridge these gaps and approximate one another's inner worlds, yet subjective experience remains fundamentally personal. We may understand another person's life intellectually, but what it is like to be them ultimately belongs to them alone.
