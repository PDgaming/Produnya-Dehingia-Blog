## Why I Wanted to Build a Neural Network

When OpenAI released ChatGPT in late 2022, I was 12 years old. Like most people, I was amazed that a computer could hold a conversation that actually felt... human. It could answer questions, explain concepts, write code, and even help me debug my own projects. But as someone who had just started learning web development, I wasn't satisfied with simply using it.

I wanted to know how it worked.

At first, my goal was simple: build my own version of ChatGPT's interface. I thought the magic was in the website itself. But while digging through tutorials, I kept running into a term that I had never really thought about before:

Model.

People kept saying things like "the model generates the responses" or "the model is trained on massive amounts of data." That confused me. What exactly was this mysterious "model" that everyone kept talking about?

And that question led me down a rabbit hole.

I spent weeks watching YouTube videos, reading articles, and trying to piece together what artificial intelligence actually was. And along the way, I discovered concepts like machine learning, neural networks, training data, weights, biases, and activation functions.

The deeper I went, the more fascinated I became.

The idea that a computer could _learn_ instead of being explicitly programmed felt almost magical. Instead of writing hundreds of if-statements to cover every possible situation, you could create a system that gradually adjusted itself until it produced the answer you wanted.

I knew I had to build one myself.

And like almost every beginner in machine learning, I started with the classic recommendation: the MNIST handwritten digit dataset.

Everyone seemed to say, "If you want to learn neural networks, start with MNIST."

So I did.

I followed tutorials, copied code, watched entire playlists explaining forward propagation and backpropagation. But none of it made sense.

The code worked, but I had no idea _why_ it worked. Every tutorial seemed to jump from a simple equation to pages of calculus and strange mathematical notation that I had never seen before. I could reproduce someone else's code, but I couldn't explain a single line of it.

And that really frustrated me.

I wasn't interested in building a neural network by blindly following a tutorial. I wanted to understand every piece of it, from the first multiplication to the final prediction.

That goal, ended up taking me four years.
